Phase 3: Build Your MVP

Getting Your First Users

40 min read
Lesson 12 of 16

You've built your MVP. Now comes the challenge that trips up most founders: getting people to actually use it. In this lesson, you'll learn scrappy, low-cost strategies to acquire your first customers and start building traction.

The First Users Challenge

Getting your first users is different from scaling. At this stage:

  • You don't have money for expensive marketing
  • You don't have brand recognition
  • You need users who will give you feedback, not just numbers
  • Every user counts‚Äîyou might talk to each one personally

Key Concept

Your first users should be early adopters‚Äîpeople who urgently need a solution and are willing to try something new. They'll be more forgiving of bugs and more likely to give valuable feedback.

The "Do Things That Don't Scale" Mindset

Y Combinator cofounder Paul Graham's famous advice: "Do things that don't scale."

In the early days, forget about automation and efficiency. Instead:

  • Manually recruit users one by one
  • Personally onboard each user
  • Send individual emails, not mass newsletters
  • Go where your users are, physically or online
All startups are initially strangers. The only way to start is by personally recruiting users one at a time.
‚Äî Paul Graham, Y Combinator

Strategies for Finding First Users

1. Your Existing Network

Start with people you already know.

  • Friends and family (but get honest feedback, not just support)
  • Former colleagues or classmates
  • People you've met through your problem interviews
  • Your social media connections

How to ask: "I've been working on [product] for [target users]. Do you know anyone who [has the problem]? I'd love to give them early access and get their feedback."

2. Online Communities

Go where your target users hang out online.

Places to look:

  • Reddit (find relevant subreddits)
  • Facebook Groups
  • Discord servers
  • Slack communities
  • Twitter/X communities
  • LinkedIn groups
  • Specialized forums

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Spam

Communities hate self-promotion. First, become a genuine member. Add value by answering questions and participating. Then, when relevant, mention your product naturally. Or reach out to individuals directly.

3. Content Marketing

Create valuable content that attracts your target users.

  • Blog posts: Answer questions your users are searching for
  • Social media: Share insights related to your problem space
  • Videos: Educational content on YouTube or TikTok
  • Podcasts: Interview experts in your space

Key principle: Give away valuable information for free. This builds trust and attracts people who have the problem you solve.

4. Building in Public

Share your journey of building your startup publicly.

  • Tweet about your progress and learnings
  • Share behind-the-scenes on Instagram/TikTok
  • Write about your wins and failures
  • Share your metrics (builds trust and interest)

Building in public attracts:

  • Potential users who relate to your mission
  • Other founders who might collaborate
  • Advisors and investors
  • Press and media attention

5. Direct Outreach

Reach out directly to potential users.

Cold email framework:

  1. Personal hook: Reference something specific about them
  2. Problem statement: Show you understand their challenge
  3. Brief pitch: One sentence about your solution
  4. Soft ask: Would they be open to trying it / giving feedback?

Example: Cold Outreach Email

Subject: Quick question about [their challenge]

Hi [Name],

I saw your post about [specific thing]. I'm building something that helps [type of person] with [problem].

Would you be open to trying it out? I'd love your honest feedback‚Äîgood or bad. As a thank you, I'll give you [free access/discount/etc.].

No worries if not!

[Your name]

6. Partnerships

Partner with people or organizations who already have your audience.

  • Influencers: Not just big names‚Äîmicro-influencers in your niche can be very effective
  • Complementary products: Products that serve the same audience but don't compete
  • Communities: Offer value to community leaders in exchange for access
  • Schools/organizations: Partner with student groups, clubs, etc.

7. Launch Platforms

Get visibility through product launch communities.

  • Product Hunt: Great for B2B and developer tools
  • Hacker News: Tech-savvy audience, be authentic
  • BetaList: For early-stage products seeking beta users
  • Reddit launch posts: In relevant subreddits (follow rules!)

Converting Visitors to Users

Getting people to your site is only half the battle. You need to convert them.

Reduce Friction

  • Minimize sign-up steps (email only to start)
  • Offer a free trial or freemium option
  • Make the first experience immediate and valuable
  • Show social proof (even early testimonials help)

Create Urgency

  • Limited beta access
  • Early adopter pricing that expires
  • "Founding member" status

The First 10, 100, 1000 Framework

Think about user acquisition in stages:

First 10 users:

  • Hand-pick them from your network
  • Personally onboard each one
  • Focus on learning, not growth

First 100 users:

  • Expand to communities and outreach
  • Start testing what messages resonate
  • Build initial word of mouth

First 1000 users:

  • Double down on what's working
  • Start building repeatable channels
  • User referrals become important

Measuring Acquisition

Track where your users come from:

  • Which channels bring the most users?
  • Which channels bring the best users (who stick around)?
  • What's your conversion rate from visitor to signup?
  • What's the cost per acquisition?
