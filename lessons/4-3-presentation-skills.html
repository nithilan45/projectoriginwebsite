Phase 4: Pitch & Launch

Presentation Skills

35 min read
Lesson 15 of 16

Your pitch is only as good as your delivery. A mediocre deck delivered with confidence beats a perfect deck delivered nervously. In this lesson, you'll learn the skills to present with impact and handle any question that comes your way.

The Fundamentals of Great Delivery

1. Know Your Material Cold

You should be able to give your pitch without slides if you had to. The slides support your story‚Äîthey don't tell it.

  • Practice until you know every transition naturally
  • Don't memorize word-for-word (sounds robotic)
  • Know key numbers and facts by heart
  • Be ready to start from any slide

2. Start Strong

The first 30 seconds set the tone. Don't waste them with "Hi, um, thanks for having me..."

Strong openings:

  • Start with a surprising statistic
  • Tell a brief, relevant story
  • Ask a provocative question
  • Make a bold statement

Example: Strong Opening

Weak: "Hi everyone, I'm going to talk about our company that helps students with college applications..."

Strong: "3.4 million first-generation students will apply to college this year. Most of them will be navigating the process completely alone. That's the problem we're solving."

3. End Memorably

Your closing is what they'll remember most. Don't fade out with "so, yeah, that's it..."

  • Summarize your key ask clearly
  • End with your vision or a call to action
  • Make your last sentence count

Body Language

Your body communicates as much as your words.

Posture

  • Stand tall, shoulders back
  • Plant your feet‚Äîdon't sway or pace nervously
  • Face the audience, not the screen

Eye Contact

  • Look at individuals, not over heads
  • Hold eye contact for 2-3 seconds, then move
  • Cover all sections of the room
  • If nervous, look at friendly faces first

Gestures

  • Use hands naturally to emphasize points
  • Avoid: crossing arms, hands in pockets, fidgeting
  • Keep gestures above waist level
  • When not gesturing, keep hands relaxed at sides

Movement

  • Move with purpose, not nervously
  • Step toward the audience for important points
  • Use the space, but don't pace

Voice Control

Pace

  • Slower than you think‚Äînervousness speeds you up
  • Vary your pace: slow for important points, faster for energy
  • Aim for 120-150 words per minute

Pauses

Pauses are powerful. Use them to:

  • Let important points land
  • Transition between sections
  • Collect your thoughts
  • Create dramatic effect

The Power of the Pause

A 2-second pause feels eternal to you but feels confident to the audience. Practice pausing‚Äîit's one of the most underused presentation tools.

Volume and Energy

  • Project‚Äîbe heard in the back row
  • Vary volume for emphasis (louder for key points)
  • Your energy is contagious‚Äîif you're excited, they'll be excited

Managing Nerves

Everyone gets nervous. Here's how to manage it:

Before the Pitch

  • Prepare relentlessly: Confidence comes from knowing your material
  • Practice out loud: In front of a mirror, friends, or recording yourself
  • Visit the space: If possible, see where you'll present beforehand
  • Physical exercise: A quick walk or workout can burn off nervous energy
  • Power poses: 2 minutes of confident posture can actually boost confidence

During the Pitch

  • Breathe: Deep breath before starting and between sections
  • Slow down: Consciously go slower than feels natural
  • Find friendly faces: Make eye contact with people who are nodding
  • Use nervousness: Reframe it as excitement‚Äîsame physical symptoms

If You Mess Up

  • Keep going‚Äîaudiences don't notice small mistakes
  • Don't apologize unnecessarily
  • Take a breath and continue
  • You can even acknowledge it briefly: "Let me rephrase that..."

Handling Q&A

Q&A is where many pitches are won or lost.

Preparing for Questions

  • Anticipate the 10 most likely questions
  • Prepare concise answers
  • Have backup slides for detailed questions
  • Practice being stumped‚Äîhow will you handle unknown questions?

Answering Framework

  1. Listen fully: Don't interrupt, even if you know where they're going
  2. Pause briefly: Shows you're thinking, not just reacting
  3. Answer directly: Start with the answer, then explain
  4. Be concise: Don't ramble‚Äî1-2 minutes max
  5. Confirm: "Does that answer your question?"

Tough Question Tactics

If you don't know:

  • "That's a great question. I don't have that data right now, but I'll follow up with you."
  • "We're still figuring that out. Here's how we're thinking about it..."

If it's hostile:

  • Stay calm and professional
  • Find the legitimate concern underneath
  • "I understand your skepticism. Here's why we believe..."

If it's off-topic:

  • "Great question. That's getting into details we could discuss after, but at a high level..."

Practice Methods

Record Yourself

Video yourself pitching and watch it back. Painful but effective. Look for:

  • Filler words (um, like, you know)
  • Nervous habits (playing with hair, rocking)
  • Pace and energy
  • Eye contact

Practice with Friends

  • Ask them to take notes on your delivery
  • Have them ask hard questions
  • Get specific feedback: "What was unclear? Where did you lose interest?"

The Mirror Method

Practice in front of a mirror. Watch your expressions and gestures.

Time Yourself

Know exactly how long your pitch takes. Add 10% buffer for live delivery.
