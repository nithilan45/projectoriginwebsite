Phase 4: Pitch & Launch

Building Your Pitch Deck

45 min read
Lesson 14 of 16

A pitch deck is your startup's story in visual form. Whether you're pitching to investors, entering a competition, or explaining your idea to partners, a great deck can open doors. In this lesson, you'll learn the structure and design principles that make pitch decks compelling.

What Makes a Great Pitch Deck?

A great pitch deck:

  • Tells a clear, compelling story
  • Can be understood in under 4 minutes
  • Is visually clean and professional
  • Addresses the key questions your audience has
  • Leaves them wanting to learn more

Key Concept

A pitch deck isn't meant to explain everything‚Äîit's meant to spark interest and start a conversation. Save the details for the Q&A or follow-up meetings.

The 10-Slide Structure

Here's a proven structure used by successful startups:

Slide 1: Title

  • Company name and logo
  • One-line description of what you do
  • Your name and contact info

Goal: Make a good first impression

Slide 2: Problem

  • What problem are you solving?
  • Who has this problem?
  • Why is it painful/important?
  • Use a story or specific example

Goal: Make them feel the pain

Slide 3: Solution

  • How do you solve the problem?
  • What's your product?
  • Show a screenshot or demo if possible
  • Keep it simple‚Äîfocus on the core value

Goal: Show you have the answer

Slide 4: Market Opportunity

  • How big is the market? (TAM/SAM/SOM)
  • What's the growth trend?
  • Why is now the right time?

Goal: Show this is worth pursuing

Slide 5: Business Model

  • How do you make money?
  • What's your pricing?
  • What are the unit economics (LTV, CAC)?

Goal: Show you can build a business, not just a product

Slide 6: Traction / Validation

  • What have you achieved so far?
  • Users, revenue, growth rate
  • Key milestones hit
  • Social proof (testimonials, press, partnerships)

Goal: Prove you're making progress

Slide 7: Competition

  • Who else is solving this problem?
  • What's your competitive advantage?
  • Use a positioning matrix to show differentiation

Goal: Show you understand the landscape and can win

Slide 8: Team

  • Who are the founders?
  • What's your relevant experience?
  • Why are you uniquely qualified?
  • Key advisors or team members

Goal: Show you can execute

Slide 9: The Ask

  • What do you need? (Investment, partnership, etc.)
  • How will you use it?
  • What will you achieve with it?

Goal: Be clear about what you want

Slide 10: Vision / Closing

  • Where is this going long-term?
  • End with an inspiring vision
  • Include contact information

Goal: Leave them excited about the future

Design Principles

Less is More

  • One idea per slide
  • No more than 6 bullets per slide
  • Use visuals over text when possible
  • Leave white space‚Äîdon't crowd slides

Visual Consistency

  • Use 2-3 colors max (from your brand)
  • Stick to 1-2 fonts
  • Keep layouts consistent slide to slide
  • Use high-quality images (not clip art)

Font Guidelines

  • Title text: 28-36pt minimum
  • Body text: 18-24pt minimum
  • Must be readable from the back of a room

Tools for Design

  • Google Slides: Free, collaborative, good enough
  • Canva: Beautiful templates, easy to use
  • Figma: More control, steeper learning curve
  • PowerPoint/Keynote: Classic options

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes

Too much text: If you have to read your slides, you've failed. Slides support your story, they don't tell it.

No clear ask: Always be clear about what you want from your audience.

Weak problem statement: If the problem doesn't resonate, nothing else matters.

The Different Deck Versions

Presentation Deck

Used when you're presenting live. Minimal text, you provide the narration.

Email/Reading Deck

Sent to be read without you present. Needs more context and explanation in the slides themselves.

One-Pager

A single page summary of your startup. Good for initial outreach.
