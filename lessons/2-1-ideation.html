Phase 2: Solution Design

Ideation & Brainstorming

30 min read
Lesson 5 of 16

You've identified and validated a problem worth solving. Now comes the fun part: generating ideas for how to solve it. In this lesson, you'll learn proven techniques to generate creative solutions and evaluate which ones are worth pursuing.

The Ideation Mindset

Before diving into techniques, let's establish the right mindset for ideation:

Key Rule

Separate idea generation from idea evaluation. First, generate as many ideas as possible without judgment. Then, evaluate them. Mixing these two phases kills creativity.

During brainstorming:

  • Quantity over quality: More ideas = better chance of finding great ones
  • No criticism: "That won't work" is banned. Every idea is valid.
  • Wild ideas welcome: Crazy ideas often lead to practical breakthroughs
  • Build on others: "Yes, and..." instead of "No, but..."

Brainstorming Techniques

1. The "How Might We" Method

Reframe your problem as a "How Might We" (HMW) question. This opens up possibility instead of focusing on constraints.

Example: Reframing as HMW

Problem: First-generation college students don't have access to application advice from people who've been through it recently.

HMW questions:

  • How might we connect students with recent graduates?
  • How might we make expert advice more accessible?
  • How might we capture and share knowledge that exists but isn't documented?
  • How might we make the application process less overwhelming?

2. The 10x Brainstorm

Force yourself to generate at least 10 different solutions. The first few will be obvious. By #7-10, you'll start thinking more creatively.

If you're stuck, try these prompts:

  • What if it was a service instead of a product? (or vice versa)
  • What if it was free? How would we make money differently?
  • What if we only served 100 people really well?
  • What would the opposite solution look like?
  • What would [successful company] do?

3. SCAMPER Method

SCAMPER helps you generate ideas by looking at existing solutions through different lenses:

  • S - Substitute: What can we substitute or swap?
  • C - Combine: What can we combine or merge?
  • A - Adapt: What can we adapt from other industries/contexts?
  • M - Modify: What can we magnify, minimize, or change?
  • P - Put to another use: How else could this be used?
  • E - Eliminate: What can we remove or simplify?
  • R - Reverse/Rearrange: What if we reversed the process?

4. Constraint-Based Ideation

Paradoxically, adding constraints can boost creativity. Try generating solutions with artificial limitations:

  • "What if we could only use text messages?"
  • "What if the entire product had to fit on one screen?"
  • "What if users could only spend 2 minutes a day on it?"
  • "What if we had to launch in one week?"
  • "What if it had to work without the internet?"

5. Reverse Brainstorming

Instead of asking "How do we solve this?", ask "How do we make this problem WORSE?" Then reverse those ideas.

Example: Reverse Brainstorming

Problem: Students are stressed about college applications.

How to make it worse:

  • Give them more confusing, contradictory advice
  • Make deadlines even more unclear
  • Remove any human support

Reversed into solutions:

  • Create a single, curated source of trusted advice
  • Build a clear timeline with all deadlines in one place
  • Provide personal mentorship from someone who's been there

Evaluating Your Ideas

Once you have a list of ideas (aim for 15-20+), it's time to evaluate them. Here are frameworks to help:

The ICE Framework

Score each idea 1-10 on three dimensions:

  • Impact: How big would the impact be if this works?
  • Confidence: How confident are you that it will work?
  • Ease: How easy is it to test or build?

The Feasibility-Desirability-Viability Test

Every good solution needs to pass three tests:

  • Desirable: Do people actually want this? (You validated this in Phase 1)
  • Feasible: Can you actually build/deliver this?
  • Viable: Can you build a sustainable business around this?

Key Concept

The best startup ideas sit at the intersection of desirability, feasibility, and viability. Missing any one of these means the idea won't work.

Questions to Ask About Each Idea

  • Does this directly address the validated problem?
  • Could I build a simple version of this quickly?
  • Would my early adopters be excited about this?
  • Do I have any unfair advantage in executing this?
  • Does this feel different from what already exists?
  • Could this scale if it works?

When to Pivot vs. Persevere

As you evaluate ideas, you might realize your initial direction isn't quite right. That's okay! Here's how to think about pivoting:

Consider pivoting if:

  • Customer interviews reveal a different, more urgent problem
  • Your idea can't pass the feasibility or viability tests
  • Someone else is doing the exact same thing much better
  • You keep finding reasons why this won't work

Persevere if:

  • Customer excitement is high
  • You have a unique angle or advantage
  • The challenges are solvable with more effort/creativity
  • You feel genuinely excited about the solution

Narrowing Down to Your Best Idea

After evaluation, you should have 2-3 promising ideas. To pick one:

  1. Sleep on it. Let your subconscious process. Which idea do you keep thinking about?
  2. Talk to potential customers. Describe each idea briefly. Which one gets the strongest reaction?
  3. Consider your motivation. Which problem are you most passionate about solving? Startups are hard‚Äîpassion helps you persist.
  4. Trust your gut. After all the analysis, sometimes you just know.
