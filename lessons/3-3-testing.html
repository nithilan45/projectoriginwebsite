Phase 3: Build Your MVP

Testing & Iteration

35 min read
Lesson 11 of 16

Building an MVP is just the beginning. The real value comes from testing it with real users, measuring what happens, and improving based on what you learn. In this lesson, you'll master the Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop.

The Build-Measure-Learn Loop

This cycle is the heart of lean startup methodology:

  1. Build: Create a small experiment (your MVP or a feature)
  2. Measure: Track what users actually do
  3. Learn: Analyze data and decide what to do next

Key Concept

The goal isn't to build‚Äîit's to learn. Every feature, every experiment should be designed to answer a specific question about your business.

What to Measure

With so many possible metrics, it's easy to get overwhelmed. Focus on what matters.

Vanity Metrics vs. Actionable Metrics

Vanity Metrics look good but don't help you make decisions:

  • Total users (doesn't tell you if they're active)
  • Page views (doesn't tell you if they're engaged)
  • Social media followers (doesn't mean they'll buy)

Actionable Metrics tell you what's working and what to change:

  • Activation rate (% who complete key action)
  • Retention rate (% who come back)
  • Conversion rate (% who buy)
  • Revenue per user

The Pirate Metrics (AARRR)

A useful framework for tracking the customer journey:

  • Acquisition: How do users find you?
  • Activation: Do they have a good first experience?
  • Retention: Do they come back?
  • Revenue: Do they pay?
  • Referral: Do they tell others?

For early-stage startups, focus on Activation and Retention first. If people aren't getting value (activation) or coming back (retention), nothing else matters.

The One Metric That Matters (OMTM)

At any given time, pick ONE metric to focus on. This keeps you focused and prevents optimization chaos.

Example: Choosing Your OMTM

Stage: Pre-launch ‚Üí Email signups (demand validation)

Stage: Just launched ‚Üí Activation rate (are people getting value?)

Stage: Have users ‚Üí Retention (are they coming back?)

Stage: Retaining users ‚Üí Revenue (will they pay?)

Running Experiments

Don't just guess‚Äîrun structured experiments to learn.

The Experiment Framework

  1. Hypothesis: "We believe [this change] will [cause this effect] for [these users]"
  2. Metric: What specific number will you measure?
  3. Target: What result would validate the hypothesis?
  4. Duration: How long will you run the experiment?
  5. Results: What actually happened?
  6. Learning: What does this tell you? What will you do next?

Example: Experiment

Hypothesis: We believe adding a progress bar will increase course completion rate for new users.

Metric: % of users who complete first module within 7 days

Target: Increase from 20% to 35%

Duration: 2 weeks, 100 new users

Result: Completion increased to 41%

Learning: Users respond well to visible progress. Keep the feature and test other motivational elements.

Collecting User Feedback

Data tells you what users do. Feedback tells you why.

Ways to Collect Feedback

  • User interviews: Deep conversations with individual users
  • Surveys: Quick questions to many users
  • Feedback forms: In-app ways to share thoughts
  • Support requests: What are people asking for help with?
  • Session recordings: Watch how users actually use your product (tools like Hotjar)
  • Social listening: What are people saying about you online?

Good Feedback Questions

  • "What were you trying to accomplish?"
  • "What was confusing or frustrating?"
  • "What's one thing you'd change?"
  • "Would you be disappointed if this product no longer existed? Why?"
  • "How would you describe this product to a friend?"

The Sean Ellis Test

Ask users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"

  • Very disappointed
  • Somewhat disappointed
  • Not disappointed

Product-Market Fit Indicator

If 40%+ of users say "very disappointed," you likely have product-market fit. Below 40%, you have more work to do.

Making Data-Driven Decisions

Once you have data and feedback, you need to act on it.

The Decision Framework

For each insight, ask:

  1. Is this a pattern or an outlier? (Look for repeated signals)
  2. Does this align with our core hypothesis?
  3. What's the cost of acting vs. not acting?
  4. What's the minimum we could do to test further?

When to Pivot vs. Persevere

Signals to persevere:

  • Users who try the product love it
  • Problems are in execution, not the core idea
  • Metrics are improving over time
  • Customers are asking for more features (not leaving)

Signals to pivot:

  • Users try it once and don't come back
  • Consistently hearing "this isn't quite what I need"
  • Can't get past a certain metric ceiling
  • Discovered a bigger/better problem to solve

Iteration in Practice

Here's what a healthy iteration cycle looks like:

  1. Weekly review: Look at your metrics. What changed? Why?
  2. Prioritize learning: What's the most important thing to learn next?
  3. Run small experiments: Test one thing at a time
  4. Talk to users regularly: At least 2-3 conversations per week
  5. Ship improvements weekly: Small, frequent updates beat big, rare launches

Don't wait for perfect data. Make decisions with the information you have, learn from the results, and adjust. Speed of learning beats quality of any single decision.
