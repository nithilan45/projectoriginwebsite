Phase 1: Problem Discovery

Validating Your Problem

35 min read
Lesson 3 of 16

You've identified a problem. But is it worth solving? Not every problem is a business opportunity. In this lesson, you'll learn how to validate that your problem is real, significant, and worth building a solution for.

Why Validation Matters

The number one reason startups fail is building something nobody wants. Founders fall in love with their idea, spend months (or years) building it, and then discover that nobody cares enough to pay for it.

Key Concept

Validation is about gathering evidence that your problem is worth solving BEFORE you invest significant time and resources into building a solution.

Think of validation like a scientist testing a hypothesis. Your hypothesis is: "This problem exists, it's painful enough that people will pay to solve it, and enough people have this problem to build a business."

The Difference Between "Nice to Have" and "Must Have"

This is one of the most important distinctions in entrepreneurship:

  • "Nice to Have" Problems: People acknowledge the problem exists but aren't actively trying to solve it. They can live with it. They'll say "that would be cool" but won't pull out their wallet.
  • "Must Have" Problems: People are actively searching for solutions. They're spending time or money trying to fix it RIGHT NOW. They feel real pain.
If you're not solving a hair-on-fire problem, you're going to have a hard time getting anyone to pay attention.
‚Äî Michael Seibel, Y Combinator

Signs you've found a "must have" problem:

  • People are already spending money on imperfect solutions
  • They've built their own workarounds (spreadsheets, manual processes)
  • They get emotional when describing the problem
  • They ask how they can get your solution before you've even built it

How to Size Your Market

A problem needs to affect enough people to build a viable business. Here's how to estimate your market size:

TAM, SAM, SOM Framework

  • TAM (Total Addressable Market): The total demand for your type of solution if you had 100% market share. This is the "big picture" number.
  • SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market): The portion of TAM you can realistically reach with your specific product and business model.
  • SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market): The portion of SAM you can realistically capture in the near term. This is your actual target.

Example: Market Sizing

Problem: College students struggle to find affordable, healthy meal options.

TAM: All college students in the US (~20 million) √ó average food spending (~$3,000/year) = $60 billion

SAM: Students at universities in major cities who actively seek healthy options (~2 million) √ó relevant spending (~$1,000/year) = $2 billion

SOM: Students at 10 target universities in your first year (~100,000) √ó your service pricing (~$200/year) = $20 million

You don't need a massive market to start. Many successful companies started with a niche and expanded. But you should have a reasonable path to a market that can support a real business.

Running a "Problem Validation Sprint"

Here's a structured approach to validate your problem in one week:

Day 1-2: Define Your Hypothesis

Write down exactly what you're trying to validate:

  • Who has this problem? (Be specific: demographics, behaviors, situation)
  • What is the problem? (In their words, not yours)
  • Why does it matter to them? (Consequences of the problem)
  • What are they doing about it now? (Current solutions or workarounds)

Day 3-5: Talk to People

Conduct 10-15 problem interviews. This is the heart of validation.

Who to talk to:

  • People who fit your target profile
  • Try to find people you don't know (friends are too polite)
  • Look in online communities, schools, workplaces where your target hangs out

What to ask:

  • "Tell me about the last time you experienced [problem]."
  • "What did you do about it?"
  • "What's the hardest part about [relevant situation]?"
  • "How often does this happen?"
  • "Have you tried to solve this? What did you try?"
  • "What would it mean to you if this was solved?"

‚ö†Ô∏è Avoid These Mistakes

Don't pitch your solution during validation. Don't ask "would you use X?" or "would you pay for Y?" People are bad at predicting their future behavior, and they'll say yes to be nice. Focus only on understanding the problem.

Day 6-7: Analyze and Decide

Look for patterns in your interviews:

  • Did multiple people describe the same problem in similar ways?
  • Did they seem emotionally affected by the problem?
  • Are they actively trying to solve it?
  • Are they spending time or money on current solutions?
  • Did any of them ask how they could get your (future) solution?

Red Flags: Signs to Pivot

Sometimes validation reveals that your problem isn't worth pursuing. That's actually a success‚Äîyou've saved yourself months of work on the wrong thing.

Pivot if you see these red flags:

  • "That's interesting, but..." ‚Äî Polite interest without excitement means it's not a real problem for them
  • Can't find people to interview ‚Äî If you can't find anyone with this problem, your market might be too small
  • No current solutions ‚Äî If people aren't trying ANYTHING to solve it, maybe it's not painful enough
  • Inconsistent descriptions ‚Äî If everyone describes the problem differently, you might be seeing multiple different problems
  • "I'd use it if it was free" ‚Äî Willingness to use something free isn't validation; everyone likes free stuff

Pivoting isn't failure‚Äîit's learning. Many successful startups pivoted multiple times before finding the right problem to solve.

Documenting Your Validation

Keep a record of your validation findings. This will help you:

  • Make objective decisions instead of going with your gut
  • Communicate your findings to co-founders, mentors, or investors
  • Refer back to customer insights as you build your solution

For each interview, record:

  • Who you talked to (anonymous is fine, but note relevant details)
  • Key quotes about the problem
  • What solutions they've tried
  • How severe the problem seems (rate 1-10)
  • Your overall impression
