40 min read
Lesson 7 of 16

A great product isn't enough‚Äîyou need a great business model. Your business model is how you create, deliver, and capture value. In this lesson, you'll learn the fundamentals of business models and how to design one that's sustainable.

What is a Business Model?

A business model describes how your company makes money. It answers questions like:

  • How do you create value for customers?
  • How do you deliver that value?
  • How do you capture some of that value as revenue?

Key Concept

Your business model is just as important as your product. A mediocre product with a great business model often beats a great product with a bad business model.

The Business Model Canvas

The Business Model Canvas, created by Alexander Osterwalder, is a one-page visual tool that maps out the 9 key elements of any business.

The 9 Building Blocks

  1. Customer Segments: Who are you creating value for?
  2. Value Propositions: What value do you deliver? (covered in previous lesson)
  3. Channels: How do you reach and deliver value to customers?
  4. Customer Relationships: What type of relationship do you maintain?
  5. Revenue Streams: How do you make money?
  6. Key Resources: What assets do you need?
  7. Key Activities: What must you do to deliver your value proposition?
  8. Key Partnerships: Who helps you?
  9. Cost Structure: What are your major costs?

Revenue Models: How Startups Make Money

There are many ways to generate revenue. Here are the most common models:

1. Direct Sales

Sell a product or service directly to customers for a one-time payment.

  • Pros: Simple, immediate revenue
  • Cons: Need to keep finding new customers
  • Examples: E-commerce stores, consulting services

2. Subscription / Recurring Revenue

Charge customers on a regular basis (monthly, annually) for ongoing access.

  • Pros: Predictable revenue, high customer lifetime value
  • Cons: Harder to get initial commitment, churn is a risk
  • Examples: Netflix, Spotify, most SaaS products

3. Freemium

Offer a free basic version and charge for premium features.

  • Pros: Low barrier to try, viral growth potential
  • Cons: Most users never pay, need huge scale
  • Examples: Dropbox, LinkedIn, Canva

4. Marketplace / Transaction Fee

Connect buyers and sellers, taking a cut of each transaction.

  • Pros: Scales well, aligned incentives
  • Cons: Chicken-and-egg problem (need both sides)
  • Examples: Airbnb, Uber, Etsy

5. Advertising

Offer free content/service, make money from advertisers.

  • Pros: Free for users, unlimited scale
  • Cons: Need massive scale, privacy concerns
  • Examples: Google, Facebook, TikTok

6. Licensing

Let others use your intellectual property for a fee.

  • Pros: Passive income, leverage others' distribution
  • Cons: Less control, need valuable IP
  • Examples: Disney characters, software licenses

Which Model Should You Choose?

Start with subscription if you can‚Äîrecurring revenue is the most valuable. Marketplaces are powerful but hard to start. Advertising requires massive scale. Choose based on your customer's willingness to pay and your ability to deliver value.

Understanding Unit Economics

Unit economics are the revenues and costs associated with a single "unit" of your business (usually one customer). Understanding this is crucial for knowing if your business can be profitable.

Key Metrics

Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC): How much does it cost to get one new customer?

CAC = Total Marketing & Sales Costs √∑ Number of New Customers

Customer Lifetime Value (LTV or CLV): How much revenue does one customer generate over their entire relationship with you?

LTV = Average Revenue per Customer √ó Average Customer Lifespan

The Golden Rule:

LTV should be at least 3x CAC

If you spend $100 to acquire a customer, that customer should generate at least $300 in revenue over their lifetime. Otherwise, you'll run out of money before becoming profitable.

Example: Unit Economics Calculation

Subscription service at $10/month:

Average customer stays 18 months

LTV = $10 √ó 18 = $180

 

Marketing spend: $5,000/month

New customers from marketing: 100/month

CAC = $5,000 √∑ 100 = $50

 

LTV:CAC Ratio = $180 √∑ $50 = 3.6x ‚úì (Healthy!)

Pricing Strategies

Pricing is one of the most important (and most underestimated) decisions you'll make.

Pricing Methods

Cost-Plus Pricing: Calculate your costs, add a markup.

  • Simple but ignores customer value

Competitor-Based Pricing: Price relative to alternatives.

  • Safe but may leave money on the table

Value-Based Pricing: Price based on the value you create for customers.

  • Most profitable but requires understanding customer value
Price is what you pay. Value is what you get.
‚Äî Warren Buffett

Pricing Tips for Startups

  • Start higher than you think: You can always lower prices; raising them is hard.
  • Test different prices: You won't know the right price without testing.
  • Price for the value you deliver: If you save someone 10 hours, charging for 1 hour of their time is reasonable.
  • Don't compete on price alone: There's always someone willing to be cheaper.

Channels: How You Reach Customers

Channels are how you communicate with and deliver value to customers. Consider:

  • Awareness: How do customers discover you? (Social media, SEO, ads, referrals)
  • Evaluation: How do they learn more? (Website, demos, reviews)
  • Purchase: How do they buy? (Online checkout, sales call, app store)
  • Delivery: How do you deliver the product/service? (Download, shipping, in-person)
  • Support: How do you help them after purchase? (Email, chat, community)

Building Your First Business Model

For your first startup, keep your business model simple:

  1. Pick one customer segment ‚Äî Don't try to serve everyone
  2. Pick one revenue model ‚Äî Master one before adding complexity
  3. Pick one main channel ‚Äî Go deep before going wide
  4. Keep costs low ‚Äî Validate before investing heavily
