Phase 3: Build Your MVP

What is an MVP?

30 min read
Lesson 9 of 16

You've designed your solution. Now it's time to build something real. But don't spend months perfecting every detail‚Äîinstead, build an MVP. In this lesson, you'll learn what an MVP really is and why building less can help you learn more.

What is an MVP?

MVP stands for Minimum Viable Product. It's the simplest version of your product that allows you to start learning from real customers.

Key Concept

An MVP is not a crappy product. It's the smallest thing you can build that delivers real value to customers and lets you test your most important assumptions.

The key words are:

  • Minimum: The smallest scope that can still be useful
  • Viable: Actually delivers value to customers (not just a demo)
  • Product: Something real that customers can use

The Lean Startup Philosophy

The MVP concept comes from the Lean Startup methodology, popularized by Eric Ries. The core idea:

The only way to win is to learn faster than anyone else.
‚Äî Eric Ries, The Lean Startup

Traditional approach: Spend months building ‚Üí Launch ‚Üí Hope customers like it

Lean approach: Build small ‚Üí Learn from customers ‚Üí Iterate ‚Üí Repeat

This is called the Build-Measure-Learn loop:

  1. Build: Create the smallest thing that tests your assumption
  2. Measure: See how customers actually respond
  3. Learn: Decide what to do next based on data

Why Build an MVP?

1. You're Probably Wrong

Most startup assumptions are wrong. The product you imagine customers want is rarely what they actually need. An MVP helps you discover this quickly and cheaply.

2. Customers Can't Tell You What They Want

People are bad at predicting their own behavior. Showing them something real gets you much better feedback than asking hypotheticals.

3. Time is Your Scarcest Resource

Every week you spend building features nobody wants is a week wasted. An MVP gets you to learning faster.

4. Perfect is the Enemy of Good

You'll never feel "ready" to launch. Waiting for perfection means waiting forever.

Famous MVPs

Even billion-dollar companies started with embarrassingly simple MVPs:

Dropbox

Before building the actual product, Drew Houston made a 3-minute video demonstrating how Dropbox would work. The video got 75,000 signups overnight‚Äîproving demand before writing a single line of sync code.

Airbnb

The founders rented out air mattresses in their own apartment and created a simple website with photos. No booking system, no payments‚Äîthey just wanted to see if anyone would actually pay to stay.

Zappos

Nick Swinmurn tested if people would buy shoes online by going to local stores, photographing shoes, and posting them on a website. When someone ordered, he'd go buy the shoes and ship them. No inventory needed.

Buffer

Joel Gascoigne created a simple landing page describing Buffer's features with a pricing page. Before building anything, he tested if people would click "buy." They did‚Äîso he built it.

Types of MVPs

1. Landing Page MVP

A simple page that describes your product and captures email signups or pre-orders.

  • Tests: Is there interest? Will people give you their email?
  • Example: Buffer's pricing page before the product existed

2. Explainer Video MVP

A video showing how your product would work.

  • Tests: Do people understand the concept? Does it excite them?
  • Example: Dropbox's demo video

3. Concierge MVP

Deliver the service manually instead of building technology.

  • Tests: Will people pay? What do they actually need?
  • Example: Manually matching users before building an algorithm

4. Wizard of Oz MVP

Looks automated to customers, but you're actually doing the work manually behind the scenes.

  • Tests: Does the product concept work?
  • Example: Zappos buying and shipping shoes manually

5. Piecemeal MVP

Combine existing tools (Typeform, Zapier, Airtable) to create your product without coding.

  • Tests: Does the workflow solve the problem?
  • Example: Using Calendly + Zoom + Stripe for a coaching service

6. Single-Feature MVP

Build one core feature really well instead of many features poorly.

  • Tests: Is the core value proposition compelling?
  • Example: Instagram started as just photo filters + sharing

Defining Your MVP Scope

The hardest part of building an MVP is deciding what to include and what to leave out.

Step 1: Identify Your Riskiest Assumption

What's the biggest unknown that could kill your startup? Your MVP should test this first.

  • Do people have this problem? (Should have tested in Phase 1)
  • Will they pay for a solution? (Test with pricing)
  • Can we actually deliver the value? (Test with a working product)
  • Will they use it repeatedly? (Test retention)

Step 2: Define the Core Value

What's the ONE thing your product must do? If you had to describe your product with one verb, what would it be?

The "One Thing" Test

Your MVP should do ONE thing well. If a customer uses your MVP and that one thing works, they should feel value. Everything else can wait.

Step 3: Cut Everything Else

Once you've identified the core value, be ruthless about cutting everything that isn't essential to delivering it.

Ask for each feature:

  • Can we launch without this? (If yes, cut it)
  • Does this test our riskiest assumption? (If no, cut it)
  • Would customers still get value without this? (If yes, cut it)
