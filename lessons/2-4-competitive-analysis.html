Competitive Analysis

35 min read
Lesson 8 of 16

Understanding your competition isn't about copying them‚Äîit's about finding where you can win. In this lesson, you'll learn how to research competitors, identify gaps in the market, and position your startup for success.

Why Competitive Analysis Matters

Many founders make two opposite mistakes:

  1. Ignoring competition: "We don't have competitors" (you always do)
  2. Obsessing over competition: Copying everything they do instead of innovating

Key Concept

Competition validates that a market exists. No competitors might mean no market. The goal isn't to avoid competition‚Äîit's to find where you can differentiate and win.

Good competitive analysis helps you:

  • Understand what customers already have access to
  • Find gaps that aren't being addressed
  • Learn from competitors' successes and mistakes
  • Position your product to stand out
  • Anticipate competitive responses

Types of Competitors

Direct Competitors

Companies that solve the same problem for the same customers in a similar way.

Example: If you're building a meditation app, Headspace and Calm are direct competitors.

Indirect Competitors

Companies that solve the same problem but in a different way, or solve a related problem.

Example: For a meditation app, yoga studios, therapy apps, or even walks in nature are indirect competitors.

Substitute Competitors

Different solutions that customers might choose instead‚Äîincluding doing nothing.

Example: Customers might just listen to music to relax instead of using a meditation app.

If you can't find any competitors, either: (1) there's no real market, (2) you haven't looked hard enough, or (3) you're defining your market too narrowly. At minimum, customers are doing SOMETHING today‚Äîeven if it's nothing.

How to Research Competitors

Step 1: Make a List

Find all relevant competitors through:

  • Google searches: Search for the problem you solve, not just your solution type
  • App stores: Search relevant categories
  • Customer interviews: Ask "What do you currently use?"
  • Review sites: G2, Capterra, TrustPilot
  • Industry reports: Look for market research
  • Startup databases: Crunchbase, AngelList
  • Social media: What are people recommending?

Step 2: Analyze Each Competitor

For each major competitor, research:

Product/Service:

  • What features do they offer?
  • What's their user experience like?
  • What are customers saying in reviews?
  • What are common complaints?

Business Model:

  • How do they make money?
  • What do they charge?
  • Who are their customers?

Marketing & Positioning:

  • What's their value proposition?
  • What channels do they use?
  • What's their brand like?

Strengths & Weaknesses:

  • What do they do well?
  • Where do they fall short?
  • What are customers complaining about?

Finding Your Competitive Advantage

Your competitive advantage is what makes you different and better for your target customers. It can come from:

1. Underserved Customer Segments

Is there a group that existing solutions don't serve well?

Example: Most task management tools are built for teams. Maybe you focus on solo creators.

2. Feature Gaps

Is there functionality that customers want but competitors don't offer?

Example: Competitors don't have mobile apps, but your customers need mobile access.

3. Experience/Interface

Can you make something significantly easier or more delightful to use?

Example: Existing solutions are clunky; you make a beautiful, intuitive interface.

4. Price Point

Can you serve the market at a significantly different price?

Example: Enterprise solutions cost $500/mo; you offer 80% of the value for $50/mo.

5. Distribution/Access

Can you reach customers in ways competitors can't?

Example: You have unique access to a community or partnership.

6. Speed/Technology

Can you leverage new technology for a better solution?

Example: Use AI to automate what competitors do manually.

Creating a Positioning Map

A positioning map visually shows where competitors sit relative to each other on two key dimensions. This helps you find open space in the market.

Example: Positioning Map

For a meal delivery service, you might map competitors on:

X-axis: Price (Low ‚Üí High)

Y-axis: Health Focus (Convenience ‚Üí Healthy)

 

‚Ä¢ McDonald's: Low price, convenience-focused (bottom-left)

Choose dimensions that matter to your customers and where you can differentiate.

Competitive Strategy for Startups

As a startup, you can't compete head-on with established players. Instead:

  1. Start narrow: Be the best solution for a specific niche before expanding
  2. Move fast: Ship features and improvements quickly while competitors are slow
  3. Focus on unmet needs: Solve problems competitors are ignoring
  4. Build relationships: Give customers personal attention big companies can't
  5. Tell a different story: Position yourself in a new category if possible
If you define the market as the sum of all your competitors' customers, you've lost already. Find the customers they're not serving.
